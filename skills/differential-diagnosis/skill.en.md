# Differential Diagnosis

Use this skill when the user describes symptoms, signs, or a clinical picture and asks what condition might be causing them.

## Goal

Produce a structured, ranked differential diagnosis and clearly separate likely causes from serious conditions that must not be missed.

## Steps

1. **Identify the chief complaint** — restate the primary symptom or concern in clinical terms.
2. **Gather key data** — onset, duration, severity, location, radiation, aggravating/relieving factors, associated symptoms, past medical history, medications, allergies, demographics, risk factors.
3. **Generate a broad differential** — list common, serious, and treatable causes without premature closure.
4. **Rank by probability and danger**:
   - **Most likely** — common conditions matching the presentation.
   - **Must not miss** — life-threatening or time-sensitive diagnoses (red flags).
   - **Reasonable alternatives** — other plausible explanations.
   - **Less likely** — rare or atypical causes.
5. **Flag red flags** — symptoms or findings that require urgent in-person evaluation or emergency services.
6. **Request missing information** — state exactly what history, exam, or test data would change the ranking.
7. **Recommend next steps** — appropriate triage, examinations, labs, imaging, or referrals.

## Output format

- **Chief complaint**: one-sentence summary.
- **Key findings**: relevant positives and negatives from the provided data.
- **Differential diagnosis**:
  | Rank | Condition | Reasoning | Red flags |
  |---|---|---|---|
- **Missing data needed**: specific questions or tests.
- **Recommended actions**: triage level and next clinical steps.

## Boundaries

- Do not give a definitive diagnosis without sufficient clinical data.
- Do not minimize red-flag symptoms.
- When uncertainty is high, say so and recommend in-person evaluation.
- Always include an emergency warning when red flags are present.
