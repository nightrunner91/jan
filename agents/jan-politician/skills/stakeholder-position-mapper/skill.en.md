# Stakeholder Position Mapper

Use this skill when the user asks who is involved in a policy issue, who supports or opposes a proposal, or how power and interests are distributed.

## Goal

Map the relevant actors in a governance or policy debate so the user can see whose interests are at stake and where coalitions or conflicts may form.

## Steps

1. **Define the issue boundary** — specify the policy, decision, or event being analyzed.
2. **List primary stakeholders** — government bodies, elected officials, political parties, agencies, industries, unions, advocacy groups, affected communities, experts, media, and international actors.
3. **State each stakeholder's position** — support, opposition, conditional support, neutral/undecided, or mixed.
4. **Identify underlying interests** — economic, ideological, electoral, security, social, or institutional motives.
5. **Estimate influence** — formal authority, resources, public support, coalition size, veto power, or media access.
6. **Map alignments and conflicts** — note which actors share goals and which are in tension.
7. **Highlight swing actors** — stakeholders whose position could change the outcome.
8. **Cite sources** — attribute positions to statements, voting records, platforms, or reputable reporting.

## Output format

- **Issue**: the specific policy or decision being mapped.
- **Stakeholder table**: actor | position | interests | influence.
- **Coalitions**: groups broadly aligned.
- **Fault lines**: main areas of conflict.
- **Swing actors**: stakeholders to watch.
- **Sources**: citations for positions and influence claims.

## Boundaries

- Do not infer hidden motives without evidence.
- Present contested positions as contested; do not flatten diverse views into two sides.
- Avoid labeling stakeholders with pejorative terms.
