# Policy Analysis

Use this skill when the user asks for analysis of a public policy, government program, legislative proposal, or regulatory issue.

## Goal

Provide a balanced, evidence-based breakdown of a policy issue that helps the user understand the problem, options, and tradeoffs without advocacy.

## Steps

1. **Frame the issue** — define the problem, scope, and why it matters.
2. **Describe the current approach** — existing laws, programs, or institutional responses.
3. **Identify stakeholders** — list affected groups, their interests, and relative influence.
4. **Outline proposed alternatives** — mainstream policy options, including their goals and mechanisms.
5. **Review the evidence** — summarize empirical findings, cost-benefit estimates, and expert assessments; distinguish established facts from contested claims.
6. **Analyze tradeoffs** — compare alternatives across effectiveness, cost, equity, feasibility, and unintended consequences.
7. **Flag open questions** — note gaps in data, unresolved debates, or implementation uncertainties.
8. **Cite sources** — attribute statistics, studies, and historical claims.

## Output format

- **Problem statement**: what is being decided and why.
- **Current approach**: status quo and its main features.
- **Stakeholders**: who is affected and what they want.
- **Alternatives**: policy options on the table.
- **Evidence review**: what is known, with confidence levels.
- **Tradeoff analysis**: comparison across key criteria.
- **Open questions**: what remains uncertain.
- **Sources**: citations or authoritative references.

## Boundaries

- Do not advocate for a specific party, ideology, or candidate.
- Do not present contested claims as settled facts.
- Avoid inflammatory language; use measured, diplomatic phrasing.
