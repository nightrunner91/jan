# International Relations Briefing

Use this skill when the user asks about a bilateral or multilateral relationship, a diplomatic dispute, treaty, alliance, or foreign-policy decision.

## Goal

Provide a concise, context-rich briefing on international relations that helps the user understand interests, constraints, and policy options without taking sides.

## Steps

1. **Define the relationship and issue** — countries/organizations involved, time frame, and the specific question.
2. **Provide historical context** — key events, treaties, or turning points that shape the current situation.
3. **Identify actor interests** — security, economic, ideological, domestic, and reputational goals of each side.
4. **Describe the institutional framework** — relevant alliances, international organizations, agreements, and legal obligations.
5. **Map the current state** — cooperation, tension, negotiation, sanctions, conflict, or stalemate.
6. **Outline policy options** — diplomatic, economic, military, legal, or multilateral tools available to decision-makers.
7. **Assess tradeoffs and risks** — effectiveness, escalation risk, legitimacy, costs, and unintended consequences.
8. **Cite sources** — rely on official statements, treaty texts, reputable news, and expert analysis.

## Output format

- **Issue**: the relationship or event being briefed.
- **Historical context**: key background and milestones.
- **Actor interests**: what each side wants.
- **Institutional framework**: alliances, organizations, treaties.
- **Current state**: where things stand now.
- **Policy options**: available diplomatic and policy tools.
- **Tradeoffs and risks**: comparison of options.
- **Sources**: citations for facts and claims.

## Boundaries

- Do not advocate for one country's policy over another's.
- Distinguish between official statements, intelligence assessments, and open-source analysis.
- Avoid inflaming tensions; use neutral, precise language for disputed territories or conflicts.
