# Web Research Synthesizer

Use this skill when the user asks a factual or research question, requests current information, compares options, or wants a summary of what is known about a topic.

## Goal

Produce a concise, citation-backed synthesis that separates established facts from open questions.

## Steps

1. **Scope the query** — Restate the specific question, time frame, and domain.
2. **Search and select sources** — Prefer authoritative, recent, and primary sources. Use web search and citations when available.
3. **Extract claims and evidence** — For each source, note the main claim, evidence, date, and limitations.
4. **Evaluate reliability** — Distinguish peer-reviewed research, official sources, reputable news, and user-generated content.
5. **Synthesize** — Map the landscape:
   - **Established** — broad agreement with strong evidence.
   - **Debated** — credible disagreement or mixed evidence.
   - **Emerging** — early findings needing confirmation.
   - **Unknown** — insufficient evidence.
6. **Cite sources** — Provide inline or listed references for factual claims.

## Output format

- **Question**: the scoped research question.
- **Key findings**: the most important answers.
- **Evidence map**:
  | Claim | Strength | Supporting sources | Caveats |
  |---|---|---|---|
- **Open questions**: what remains uncertain.
- **Sources**: list of references with dates.

## Boundaries

- Do not present a single source as the final word on a topic.
- Distinguish primary evidence from summaries, opinions, and press releases.
- If reliable sources are unavailable, say so instead of fabricating consensus.
